# EHCP Phase Transfers: Navigating Life's Big Transitions

## Course: EHCP Mastery Professional Suite
## Module: Phase Transfers
## Lesson: Supporting Children Through Educational Transitions
## Duration: 4-5 minutes
## Instructor: Dr. Scott Ighavongbe-Patrick

---

## Script

[WARMTH] Think about the biggest transitions in your own life. First day at a new school. Starting secondary. Leaving for college or work.

[PAUSE]

[THOUGHTFUL] Remember how it felt? The uncertainty. The excitement mixed with fear. Not knowing where anything was, who anyone was, what the rules were.

[SLOW] Now imagine facing that with additional needs. Imagine your brain processes change differently. Imagine you rely on routines that are about to disappear. Imagine you've spent years building relationships with staff who understand you—and now you're starting over.

[CONCERN] Phase transfers are the highest-risk moments in any child's EHCP journey. More things go wrong during transitions than at any other time.

[ENERGY] And that's exactly why we built the Phase Transfers module. Not just to track deadlines—though it does that—but to ensure no child falls through the gaps when they're most vulnerable.

**Understanding the Phases**

[THOUGHTFUL] In England, there are four main educational phases, and three major transfers:

- **Early Years to Primary** (typically age 4-5)
- **Primary to Secondary** (Year 6 to Year 7, age 10-11)
- **Secondary to Post-16** (Year 11 to college/sixth form, age 15-16)

Each has different statutory timelines. Different complexities. Different stakeholders.

[SERIOUS] And here's what the law says. For primary to secondary transfer, the final EHCP must be issued by 15th February in Year 6. For post-16 transfer, by 31st March in Year 11.

[PAUSE]

[CONCERN] Miss those dates, and families don't have placement certainty. Children start new phases without confirmed provision. Trust breaks down.

**The Visual Workflow**

[ENERGY] When you open the Phase Transfers module, you see something different from traditional lists. You see a visual workflow—think of it like a Gantt chart, but designed for children, not construction projects.

Each child in a transfer year has their own track. Milestones marked: initial review scheduled, parental preferences gathered, placement consultations sent, responses received, final EHCP issued.

[WARMTH] You can see at a glance: who's on track, who's behind, who's stuck waiting for a local authority response that hasn't come.

**The Early Years Challenge**

[THOUGHTFUL] Early Years transfers are special. Often, these are children whose needs are newly identified. Parents who are just beginning to understand what EHCP processes mean. Schools who haven't met the child yet.

The module prompts early engagement. Multi-agency meetings in autumn term. School visits while the child is still in nursery. Graduated transition plans—because for some children, one orientation day isn't enough. They might need ten visits. Twenty. Whatever it takes.

[WARMTH] We've built in templates for visual social stories. "This is my new school." "This is the classroom I'll be in." "This is my new teacher." Shareable with families so children can practice at home.

**Primary to Secondary: The Big One**

[ENERGY] Year 6 to Year 7. The transfer that everyone dreads.

[PAUSE]

New building. New timetable—not one classroom but eight different subjects. New faces everywhere. Lockers. Labs. Lunch queues that never end. Friendship groups that reform and fracture.

[SLOW] For children with additional needs, this can be overwhelming. The module helps you plan not just the administrative transfer, but the human one.

Transition visit schedules. Key adult identification at the new school. Sensory audits of new environments. Peer buddy arrangements. Staff training on the child's specific needs—before September, not after.

[THOUGHTFUL] And critically, information transfer. What the new SENCO needs to know isn't just what's in the EHCP document. It's which strategies actually work. What calms this child. What triggers them. The unwritten knowledge that makes the difference.

**Post-16: Preparing for Adulthood**

[WARMTH] Post-16 transfers are different again. We're not just talking about educational placement. We're talking about preparing for adulthood.

The SEND Code of Practice requires that from Year 9—age 13-14—annual reviews should include preparation for adulthood discussions. Employment. Independent living. Community inclusion. Health.

[SERIOUS] But let's be honest. In many schools, that doesn't really happen until Year 11. And by then, there's not enough time.

The module flags preparation for adulthood requirements automatically. Year 9 onwards, annual review templates change. Outcome categories shift. The conversation broadens.

[ENERGY] By the time a young person reaches their actual post-16 transfer, you've had four years of structured preparation. Their EHCP outcomes genuinely reflect what matters for their adult life.

**Placement Consultations**

[THOUGHTFUL] One of the most stressful aspects of phase transfers is placement. Parents want specific schools. Schools have capacity constraints. Local authorities mediate.

The module tracks consultations in real-time. Request sent to School A. Response: cannot meet needs (reason: no speech therapist capacity). Request sent to School B. Response: can meet needs, offer made. Parent accepts/declines.

[WARMTH] You can see the whole journey. When decisions seem to drag, you know exactly where the bottleneck is. Is it the school? The LA? The parent considering options?

**Summer Transition Support**

[SLOW] Here's something that matters enormously but often gets forgotten. The summer holiday.

Six weeks with no school. No routine. And then—boom—new phase. For many children, that's too much change stacked on more change.

[ENERGY] The module helps you plan summer transition support. Additional visits in July. Contact maintained over summer—even if just emails with photos of the new school. Phased start arrangements in September.

[WARMTH] One school I worked with created "summer social stories"—weekly updates sent to transitioning children showing what would be happening at their new school, who was getting classrooms ready, how excited teachers were to meet them.

The engagement that built transformed September starts.

**Closing**

[THOUGHTFUL] Transitions are windows—windows of vulnerability, but also windows of opportunity. A well-managed transfer sets the tone for years to come.

[PAUSE]

[WARMTH] I've seen children utterly transformed by good transitions. Arriving at secondary school confident, relationships already forming, routines already understood. No September crisis. No December breakdown. Just steady progress.

[ENERGY] That's what's possible when you plan properly, track systematically, and put the child—not the paperwork—at the centre.

[SLOW] The Phase Transfers module won't walk alongside every child through the school gates on their first day. But it will ensure that when they do walk through, everything that should have happened, has happened.

[WARMTH] Next up: Compliance and Risk. Let's talk about how AI can predict problems before they become crises.
