# The Neurobiology of Dyslexia

**Speaker:** Dr. Scott Ighavongbe-Patrick
**Duration:** 90 seconds
**Audience:** Teachers, SENCOs, Parents

---

## Script

Let's clear up a common myth right now. Dyslexia is not about seeing letters backwards. It's not a vision problem. It is a language processing difficulty rooted deep in the brain's wiring.

I'm Dr. Scott, and I want to take you inside the dyslexic brain.

Neuroscience shows us that in a typical reader, the left hemisphere of the brain is a hive of activity. Specifically, the areas responsible for breaking words down into sounds—phonemes.

In a dyslexic brain, these areas are underactive.

This is what we call the 'Phonological Deficit Hypothesis'.

Imagine trying to build a wall, but your bricks keep crumbling. For a dyslexic child, the 'bricks' of language—the individual sounds—are fuzzy. They struggle to segment 'cat' into 'c-a-t'.

Because this automatic processing is blocked, the brain tries to compensate. It uses the right hemisphere. It uses context. It works ten times harder to achieve the same result.

That's why these children are exhausted by 11am.

But here's the good news. The brain is plastic. With the right intervention—Structured Literacy—we can actually rewire these neural pathways.

We can't 'cure' dyslexia, because it's not a disease. But we can build a bridge over the gap.
